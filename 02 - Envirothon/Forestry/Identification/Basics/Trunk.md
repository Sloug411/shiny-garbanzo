There are five main layers of a tree, the Outer Bark, Inner Bark, Cambium cell layer, Sapwood, and Heartwood.

**Outer Bark**: This section contains inactive cells which protect the Inner Bark from outside dangers. It also helps retain moisture levels during dry seasons.

**Inner Bark (Phleom)**: This section's purpose is to carry food made in the leaves downwards towards the Cambium and storage cells. It has a very short lifespan. After it dies, it becomes the outer layer of bark.

**Cambium**: This section is unique, as it is the section of the trunk that grows. Stem cells within this area divide to become bark and wood.

**Sapwood**: This section carries food and water upwards.

**Heartwood**: These are inactive cells that act as structure within the tree. Heartwood cells are dead, but they do not decay unless outer layers are damaged. It is made up of lignin. Heartwood is often the strongest part of the trunk.

There are a couple of other important parts of the trunk, such as the Pith, Medullary Rays, and Annual Rings.

**Pith**: This section is soft and in the middle of the trunk. It serves the purpose of storing and transporting nutrients throughout the tree.

**Medullary Rays**: They serve as pathways for nutrients to spread throughout a tree. They are perpendicular to Growth Rings. The rays do not appear in all species.

**Growth Rings**: They indicate how old a tree is, generally with one year per Growth Ring.
